Study at a Glance

An endline follow-up survey of home-based garment workers (wives) and their husbands across households in Pakistan. The study examines changes in women's employment, earnings, household decision-making, time use, and psychological wellbeing — with separate matched instruments for wives and husbands, enabling cross-perspective analysis of the same household.

Wife's Main Concerns about Outside Employment

Top concern raised by wives who considered or discussed outside work (n=14)
High Retention in Home-Based Work: 86% of wives (12/14) are currently working in home-based garment jobs, predominantly stitching (93%). Working wives average 5.7 days/week and 5.1 hours/day — a significant labour contribution while remaining in the household setting.
Low Aspiration for Outside Employment: Only 21% of wives (3/14) considered working outside the home in the past 12 months, and only 29% (4/14) ever discussed the option with their husband. The predominant concerns are household chores (43%) and childcare (43%) — structural barriers to formal employment.

Large Earnings Gap — Home vs Factory: Wives currently earn an average of PKR 5,208/month from home-based piecework, while they estimate that women doing the same work inside factories earn PKR 24,786/month — nearly 5× more. This gap highlights the significant wage penalty of home-based work and is a key motivator being tested by the study intervention.
Financial Autonomy is Strong: 100% of wives receive their payment directly (not via husband). Both husband and wife perspectives agree: 50–58% of wives keep all their earnings. Large Domestic Time Burden on Women: Wives spend an average of 3.79 hours/day on household chores and 2.43 hours/day on caregiving — a combined 6.2 hours of unpaid domestic work daily. This is on top of their paid home-based garment work (5.1 hrs/day), meaning working wives face a total daily workload of over 11 hours.
Husbands Contribute Little to Domestic Work: Husbands report spending only 0.5 hours/day on household chores and 0.86 hours helping their wife with chores. However, they report 2.5 hours/day on caregiving, suggesting some childcare involvement. This unequal division of household labour is a key structural barrier to women's formal employment uptake.

Strong Joint Decision Making: Both husbands and wives overwhelmingly report joint decision-making across all three domains. Healthcare and family visit decisions are 100% joint per wives, and 86–93% per husbands. This finding suggests a relatively collaborative household structure — though the large purchases domain shows more husband authority (29% husband-only per wife report).
Perspective Divergence on Purchases: While 86% of husbands report joint large-purchase decisions, only 71% of wives do — with 29% of wives saying the husband decides alone. This 15-percentage-point gap between perspectives on financial decisions is the largest cross-perspective discrepancy observed, and warrants monitoring as an indicator of women's economic agency.

Decision making variables: decision_making_1 (healthcare), decision_making_2 (large household purchases), decision_making_3 (visits to family/relatives). Positive Wellbeing Overall: Both wives and husbands show low depression and low interest loss (mh_1 and mh_2 both close to 1 = "Not at all"). Positive wellbeing indicators (happiness mh_4 and life satisfaction mh_5) score higher — wives report slightly higher satisfaction (3.29) than husbands (3.00). The higher fatigue scores (mh_3) for both groups reflect the demanding physical workload.
Harassment Widely Perceived as Common: 86% of husbands and 64% of wives report that harassment is "somewhat common" for women travelling to work. The higher husband-reported rate may reflect men's greater exposure to public discourse on this topic, or response bias in the wife sample. This shared perception of risk is a key barrier to women's outside employment uptake regardless of source.
